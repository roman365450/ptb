Why Past Tense of Banana?

Rethinking how we learn languages

My name is Gari, and I'm here to tell you that the way we've been taught to learn languages is broken.

For decades, the traditional approach has treated language like a dusty museum exhibit—something to be analyzed, dissected, and studied theoretically. Most courses are still obsessed with rigid grammatical rules and memorizing long lists of vocabulary in total isolation. They treat language like History or Mathematics.

But a language isn't a collection of facts to be stored in a drawer; it's a living, breathing tool.

A Language is Not "Studied" — It is Learned

At Past Tense of Banana, I believe a language is a skill, not a subject.

Think about it: you don't learn to ride a bike by reading a manual on physics, and you don't learn to swim by studying the chemical composition of water. You learn by doing. You learn by practicing regularly, making mistakes, and finding your rhythm.

Breaking the Labels: No "Beginners" Here

One of the biggest mistakes traditional methods make is trying to put people into boxes: Beginner, Intermediate, or Advanced. At Past Tense of Banana, I do not categorize your ability.

Those labels are outdated. I believe they belong in the past. We don't live in the world of 40 or 50 years ago, where the only way to hear English was through a rigid textbook or a single teacher in a quiet classroom. Today, the world is connected. People everywhere pick up English words and expressions from movies, video games, social media, and music. Your knowledge is a unique "mosaic," not a level on a chart. I don't care about what "level" you are; I care about how you use what you know.

Context, Fun, and the "Quirky" Factor

The name Past Tense of Banana reflects my philosophy: language should be unexpected, fun, and rooted in context.

I move away from the obsession with "perfect" grammar and focus on the experience. When you learn through stories, games, and quirky methods, your brain stops trying to "solve" the language like a puzzle and starts absorbing it naturally.

I don't want you to be an expert on the theory of English. I want you to live it, play with it, and speak it with confidence.

— Gari
